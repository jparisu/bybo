How to Build Your Brand Online

A step-by-step guide to launching your digital presence

Step 1 of 9
Optional · later

Define what your website is about. Whether it's a portfolio, business site, blog, or e-commerce store — knowing your focus helps shape every decision that follows. Consider your audience, your goals, and what success looks like for you.

  • Identify who your website is for — your audience.
  • Decide its main purpose: portfolio, business, blog, or shop.
  • Summarise it all in one clear sentence.
Tip

Write this sentence down. Every later choice — name, colors, style — should trace back to it.

Your brand name is your digital identity. Make it memorable, easy to spell, and available as a domain. Use our AI-powered name generator to explore creative options that resonate with your vision and stand out in your industry.

  • Brainstorm at least 10 candidate names.
  • Check that the matching domain (.com / .io) is free.
  • Say each one out loud — is it easy to spell and remember?
Tip

Shorter is almost always better. Avoid hyphens and numbers — they're easy to mistype.

Colors evoke emotion and convey personality. Select a palette that reflects your brand's essence — bold and energetic, calm and trustworthy, or modern and minimal. Our AI suggests harmonious color schemes based on your industry and preferences.

  • Pick one primary accent color that captures your personality.
  • Add a neutral background — light or dark — for contrast.
  • Limit yourself to 2–3 colors total.
Tip

Pick one main accent color and let it lead. Keep the whole palette to two or three colors — more than that quickly starts to look messy.

Define the visual language of your site. Minimalist or bold? Classic or avant-garde? Browse through curated style templates or let our AI analyze your preferences to recommend the perfect aesthetic direction for your brand.

  • Choose a vibe: minimal, bold, classic, or playful.
  • Pick one font family and stick to it.
  • Decide on spacing: airy and roomy, or tight and dense.
Tip

Consistency beats variety. Reusing the same spacing and font sizes is what makes a site look professional.

Use an AI app builder like Bolt to turn your inputs into a real website. Feed it your topic, name, colors and style — but be explicit that you want a static site: plain HTML, CSS and JavaScript only. When you're happy, download the project so you can publish it yourself.

  • Tell the builder to make a static site — only HTML, CSS and JS, with no React, no framework and no build step.
  • Review the generated layout, then iterate with feedback until it matches your vision.
  • Download the finished project (Bolt has a Download button that saves a .zip), then unzip it on your computer.
Important — keep it static

Builders like Bolt often default to React. You want plain static files instead. Your finished folder should hold index.html, style.css, script.js and a resources folder — with no package.json or src/ folder. If you see those, ask the builder to "rebuild as plain static HTML, CSS and JS only — no React, no build step." A real static site opens just by double-clicking index.html.

Fine-tune your design with AI assistance. Adjust layouts, swap images, refine copy, and add interactive elements. Simply describe what you want in plain English, and watch the changes happen in real-time. No coding required.

  • Describe changes in plain English ("make the hero darker").
  • Swap placeholder images and text for your real content.
  • Preview on both desktop and mobile widths.
Tip

Change one thing at a time, so you can see exactly what each tweak does.

Editing your own files is where it all comes together. Open your project folder in VS Code — a free, friendly code editor — and tweak the HTML, CSS and JavaScript directly. No terminal needed: everything happens through menus and buttons.

  • Download and install VS Code — it's free.
  • Open your project with File → Open Folder, then pick your website folder.
  • Install the Live Preview extension to watch your site update as you type.
Tip

Save often. With Live Preview open beside your code, every change shows up instantly — no need to refresh the browser.

Now put your files online so they're safe and ready to publish. GitHub stores your project for free — and you can upload everything without ever touching a terminal. Use the website's drag-and-drop, or the GitHub Desktop app.

  • Create a free account at github.com and click New to make a repository.
  • Easiest way: open your repo and choose Add file → Upload files, then drag in your index.html, style.css, script.js and the resources folder.
  • Prefer an app? Install GitHub Desktop, add your project folder, and click Publish — no commands required.
Tip

Make sure index.html sits at the top level of the repository — that's the page your website will open on.

Turn your repository into a real, public website with GitHub Pages — free hosting, no terminal and no cost. A few clicks in your repository settings and your site is live for the world to see.

  • In your repository, open Settings → Pages.
  • Under Build and deployment, set Source to "Deploy from a branch".
  • Pick the main branch and the / (root) folder, then click Save.
  • Wait a moment — your site goes live at https://<username>.github.io/<repo>/.
Tip

It can take a minute or two the first time. Refresh the page if your site isn't there yet.

GitHub Pages is perfect for a site made of HTML, CSS and JavaScript. The day your project needs to do more — handle form submissions, user logins, or a database — you'll want a host that can run server code. That's a project for another day, but here's where to look.

  • Keep using GitHub Pages until you actually need a server.
  • When you do, hosts like Netlify or Vercel can run backend code and connect to a database.
  • Both have free tiers and link straight to your GitHub repository.
Tip

Don't reach for a server too early. Most personal and brand sites never need one.
